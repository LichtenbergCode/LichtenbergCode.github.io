# Non-Technical Interview Guide

## Mindset

Your life is **not over** if you do not pass the interview.  
This is **not your only chance**. There will always be other opportunities at other companies.

Do your best, but **do not expect anything in return**.  
Detach yourself from the outcome and focus on performing well.

**Focus on impact, not activity.**  
Doing fewer things well is better than doing many things poorly.

Remember:
- The interviewer is a person too.
- They are not trying to trick you.
- They are doing their job and looking for someone they want to work with.

Be calm, be curious, and transmit good energy.

---

## What Do You Need to Demonstrate?

Every behavioral interview is trying to answer **three fundamental questions**:

1. **Can you do the job?**
2. **Can I work with you?**
3. **Are you going to grow and improve?**

Your answers should consistently reinforce these three points.

---

## The 4 Heroes Framework

Prepare **four core stories** that you can adapt to different questions:

1. **Technical Hero**  
   Demonstrates that you are technically capable and can solve problems.

2. **Success Hero**  
   Shows that you deliver results and create impact.

3. **Leadership Hero**  
   Proves that you can influence, collaborate, and take responsibility.

4. **Challenge Hero**  
   Shows how you deal with uncertainty, failure, or difficult situations.

These four stories should collectively answer the three fundamental questions above.

---
### The 4 Heroes — Example Answers

#### 1. Technical Hero (Can you do the job?)
**Question is answers:** 
> Tell me about a technical problem you solved. 

**Answer:** 
> Situation:  <br>
> I was working on a drone control system where altitude control was unstable and oscillated under certain conditions. <br><br> 
> Task:<br>
> My responsibility was to identify the root cause and stabilize the system. <br><br>
> Result: <br>
> The oscillations were eliminated, and altitude error was reduces by around 40%. The system became stable enough to be reused for the future experiments. <br><br> 
> What this shows: <br>
> I can debug complex systems, work methodically, and improve reliability through data-driven decisions. <br><br>

#### 2. Success Hero (Do you deliver impact?)

**Question is answers:**
> Tell me about a project your proud of. 

**Answer:**
> Situation: <br>
> I was developing a multi-drone system where one drone needed to follow another using visual feedback.<br><br>
> Task: <br>
> The goal was to design a working follower behavior that was robust enough to run in real time. <br><br>
> Action: <br>
> I split the system into perception, control, and communication components using ROS2, I prioritized simplicity over complexity to make debugging easier and focused on end-to-end reliability instead of adding extra features. <br><br>
> Result: <br>
> The follower drone was able to maintain stable tracking in real time, and the architecture made is easy to extend the system later with additional behaviors. <br><br>
> What this shows:  <br>
> I focus on delivering working solutions with measurable results, not just prototypes.  <br><br>


#### 3. Leadership Hero (Can I work with you?)
**Question it answers:**
> Tell me about a time you influenced other or took leadership. 

**Answer:**
> Situation:<br> 
> I was working on a project where different team members had conflicting ideas about how complex the system design should be. <br><br>
> Task:<br>
> I wasn’t the official leader, but I wanted to help the team move forward.<br><br>

> Action:<br>
> I proposed building a simple initial version first and validating assumptions with real data before adding complexity. I listened to concerns, summarized trade-offs, and aligned the discussion around the project goals.<br><br>

> Result: <br>
> The team agreed on a simpler design, delivered earlier, and avoided weeks of unnecessary rework. <br><br>

> What this shows: <br>
> I lead through collaboration, clarity, and alignment—not authority. <br><br>

#### 4. Challenge Hero (Are you going to grow?)

**Question it answers:**
> Tell me about a difficult challenge or failure.

**Answer:**
> Situation: <br>
> Early in a project, I underestimated the complexity of integrating hardware and software components.<br><br>

> Task:<br>
> I needed to fix delays and stabilize the system under time pressure.<br><br>

> Action:<br>
> I took responsibility, broke the problem into smaller pieces, and improved my debugging workflow. I also started documenting assumptions and testing components in isolation.<br><br>

> Result:<br>
> The system became more predictable, and in later projects I avoided the same mistake by planning integration earlier. <br><br>

> What this shows:<br>
> I learn from mistakes and turn them into long-term improvements.<br><br>

## “Tell Me About Yourself”

You have limited time. Use it intentionally.

Your goal is to **convince the interviewer that you are a strong candidate for this role**.

Guidelines:
- Connect your past experiences directly to the position.
- Show how each step in your journey prepared you for this role.
- Emphasize continuous growth and learning.

Include:
- Your main drivers of success
- Skills that are relevant to the job
- Topics you *want* the interviewer to ask about

Avoid:
- Unrelated personal history
- Long timelines without clear purpose

## “Why Do You Want to Work Here?”

This question is about **intentionality**.

You should:
- Show that you researched the company
- Demonstrate genuine interest
- Make the interviewer feel that this role and this company matter to you

Key points to highlight:
- Why this company aligns with your growth
- Why your skills are a good fit
- Why this role makes sense at this stage of your career

---

## “Why Did You Leave Your Previous Job?”

Always keep this **positive and forward-looking**.

- Do not complain
- Do not blame people or the company
- Focus on growth, learning, and alignment

The interviewer wants to know if you are someone people enjoy working with.

---

## “Tell Me About a Problem You Solved”

Prepare for this. It will almost always come up.

Use the **STAR method**:

- **Situation** – What was the context?
- **Task** – What was your responsibility?
- **Action** – What did you do?
- **Result** – What was the outcome?

Be **specific** and **measurable**.

Example:
- ❌ “I improved the performance of a system.”
- ✅ “I improved system performance by 50%, reducing response time from 200 ms to 100 ms.”

Facts and numbers are harder to question and easier to trust.

Focus on:
- Impact
- Trade-offs
- Performance, scalability, or reliability when applicable

People want to work with problem solvers, not complainers.

---

## “What Is Your Biggest Weakness?”

Never give fake weaknesses like:
- “I work too hard”
- “I care too much”

These sound dishonest.

Instead:
- Give a real weakness
- Explain how it affected you
- Show clearly how you worked to improve it

The goal is to demonstrate **self-awareness and growth**.

---

## “Do You Have Any Questions for Me?”

Always say yes.

Focus on **them**, not on yourself.

Good questions:
1. What is the biggest mistake you made during your first months here, and what did you learn from it?
2. Where do you see this team or company in the next three years?
3. Why did you personally choose to join this company?
4. How have your skills grown over the past year?
5. What do you wish someone had told you when you first started here?

## Tell Me About a Failure

- Take ownership
- Avoid blaming others
- Focus on what you learned
- Explain what you changed afterward

Failure is only bad if you learned nothing from it.

---

## Tell Me About a Disagreement

- Disagree respectfully
- Use data and reasoning
- Align with team goals
- Commit to the final decision, even if it was not yours

---

## Secret Weapon

- Emphasize simplicity over complexity
- Premature optimization is the root of all evil
- Think about the overall goal, not just local optimization
- Do not complain about clients, code, or teammates
- No ego
- Strong opinions, weakly held

---

## Most Common Questions

- Tell me about a problem you solved
- Why do you want to work here?
- Tell me about yourself
- What is your biggest weakness?
- Tell me about a failure
- Tell me about a disagreement
- Do you have any questions for me?

## After the Interview

- Thank the interviewer
- Reflect on what went well
- Write down improvement points for next time
